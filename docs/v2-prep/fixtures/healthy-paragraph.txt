Antibiotic resistance evolves predictably under selection pressure. When a
bacterial population is exposed to a sub-lethal antibiotic concentration,
individuals carrying resistance mutations survive at higher rates and
reproduce, increasing the resistant fraction in the next generation. This
mechanism, established in the foundational work of Luria and Delbrück
(1943), explains why hospital infections often involve multi-resistant
strains: the hospital environment maintains continuous selection. If
antibiotic use ceases, the resistant fraction may decline, but only when
resistance mutations carry a measurable fitness cost in the absence of the
drug. Recent work by Andersson and Hughes (2010, Nature Reviews
Microbiology) shows that compensatory mutations frequently restore fitness
in resistant strains, which means resistance can persist long after
selection ends. The clinical implication is that rotating antibiotics is
not by itself sufficient to reverse resistance once it is established in a
population.
